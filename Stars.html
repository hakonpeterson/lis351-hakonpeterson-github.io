Stars in Our Universe

Astronomers estimate that the universe could contain up to one septillion stars – that’s a one followed by 24 zeros. Our Milky Way alone contains more than 100 billion stars, including our most well-studied star, the Sun.

Birth of Stars

Stars form in large clouds of gas and dust called molecular clouds. These cold clouds clump together, creating high-density pockets. Eventually, gravity causes some of these clumps to collapse, leading to the development of protostars – baby stars.

As the protostar contracts, it heats up and begins nuclear fusion. Hydrogen atoms combine to form helium, releasing energy in the process. This marks the birth of a new star.

Life of Stars

Main sequence stars, like our Sun, undergo nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium. This phase lasts millions or billions of years. A star’s properties change as it ages, and its luminosity, size, and temperature evolve over time.

Some stars, especially massive ones, go through spectacular phases like red giants, supernovae, and neutron star formation. These events shape the cosmos and enrich it with heavy elements.

Death of Stars

At the end of a star’s life, its core runs out of hydrogen to convert into helium. The energy produced by fusion creates pressure inside the star, balancing gravity’s pull. The core then collapses, leading to various outcomes such as white dwarfs, neutron stars, or even black holes.

Supernovae occur when massive stars explode, scattering their enriched contents into space. These remnants contribute to the formation of new stars and planetary systems.
